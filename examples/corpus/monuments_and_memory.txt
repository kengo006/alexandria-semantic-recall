===== page 1 =====
Public monuments are commonly read as expressions of collective memory, but the
relationship runs mostly the other way: monuments are instruments through which
particular groups attempt to fix an official version of the past in shared
space. A statue does not record what a community remembers; it proposes what
the community ought to remember, and its placement, scale, and inscription are
arguments in stone.

Because of this, controversies over monuments are rarely about history in the
documentary sense. They are conflicts over present identity conducted in the
vocabulary of the past. When a monument is contested, defenders typically appeal
to heritage and continuity, while critics point to the selective silences the
monument enforces — the events and people it renders invisible.

===== page 2 =====
Commemoration, in this analysis, is a practice rather than a state: memory work
that must be repeated to remain effective. Anniversaries, wreath-layings, and
renamings are maintenance operations on the official past. When the maintenance
stops, monuments drift into what one line of scholarship calls invisibility —
physically present, semantically inert.

The digital turn complicates the picture. Online commemoration is cheap,
participatory, and archivable, which lowers the barrier for counter-memories to
organize. The same platforms, however, make official narratives easier to
amplify. Whether digital memory cultures ultimately pluralize or homogenize the
remembered past remains an open empirical question.
