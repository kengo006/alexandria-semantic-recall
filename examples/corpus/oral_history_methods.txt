===== page 1 =====
Oral history is the practice of recording, preserving, and interpreting the
spoken testimony of people who lived through historical events. Unlike archival
documents, oral testimony is co-produced in the interview situation: the
questions asked, the rapport established, and the time elapsed since the events
all shape what is remembered and how it is told. Methodologically, this means
an oral history interview is never a transparent window onto the past; it is a
memory artifact produced in the present.

The reliability of testimony has therefore been a central debate in the field.
Early critics dismissed oral sources as anecdotal and error-prone compared to
written records. Practitioners answered that the distortions of memory are
themselves historical evidence: what a community misremembers, embellishes, or
silences reveals how the past matters to it now.

===== page 2 =====
Interview methodology developed to work with, rather than against, these
properties of memory. Standard practice records the interview in full, prepares
a verbatim transcript, and treats the recording as the primary source while the
transcript serves as a finding aid. Quotation discipline follows from this
hierarchy: a quoted passage should be verified against the recording, not
reconstructed from notes taken during the interview, because interviewer notes
compress and paraphrase in ways that silently alter meaning.

Digital platforms have changed collection and access, but the epistemological
questions remain the same: whose memories are collected, who controls the
archive, and how later users can trace an interpretation back to the moment of
testimony it claims to rest on.
